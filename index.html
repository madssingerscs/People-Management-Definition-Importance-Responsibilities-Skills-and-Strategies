What is people management?

People management is the strategic process of overseeing and guiding employees to ensure they are productive, motivated, and aligned with organizational goals; it encompasses hiring, training, employee development, performance management, and team motivation.

Research by the Society for Human Resource Management (SHRM) highlights that effective people management can lead to a 21% increase in profitability and a 20% increase in productivity (SHRM, 2023). Showing the importance of ensuring that employees are skilled, competent, engaged, and satisfied in their roles.

A strong people management strategy fosters a positive organizational culture, improves team communication, and aligns individual and team goals with broader business objectives. According to a report in 2023, organizations with high employee engagement levels experience 59% less turnover than those with low engagement, directly impacting talent retention, reduced turnover, and enhanced team performance.

Who is a People Manager?

A People Manager, also known as a Human Resource Manager, Team Leader, Supervisor, Personnel Manager, or Talent Manager, is a professional responsible for overseeing and guiding employees within an organization. They possess attributes such as being strategic, empathetic, organized, motivational, communicative, decisive, observant, supportive, authoritative, detail-oriented, approachable, analytical, creative, resourceful, and proactive. Their values include employee well-being, organizational alignment, compliance, team cohesion, goal achievement, leadership, productivity, quality control, mentorship, workforce planning, employee satisfaction, efficiency, talent acquisition, career development, and retention.

According to a 2023 evidence review by the Chartered Institute of Personnel and Development (CIPD), effective people management can lead to a 21% increase in employee productivity and a 19% decrease in turnover. Closely related entities to People Managers are HR policies, recruitment, employee relations, team meetings, performance reviews, project management, daily operations, task delegation, employee feedback, payroll, benefits administration, workforce analytics, talent pipelines, training programs, and succession planning.

What Are the Core Skills and Qualities That Define an Effective People Manager?

Core skills that define effective people managers include:

  • Emotional Intelligence (EQ): The ability to understand and manage one’s emotions while effectively recognizing and responding to the feelings of others.
  • Effective Communication: Clear and transparent communication fosters trust and ensures alignment on organizational goals.
  • Adaptability: Flexibility in adjusting strategies and approaches based on team needs and changing circumstances.
  • Conflict Resolution Skills: Managing disagreements constructively to maintain a cohesive and collaborative team environment.
  • Empathy: Demonstrating genuine care and understanding for employees’ personal and professional challenges.
  • Coaching and Mentoring: Guiding employees toward growth and development by providing feedback, encouragement, and support.
  • Decision-Making Skills: Making timely and informed decisions that align with organizational objectives.
  • Accountability: Holding oneself and team members responsible for meeting expectations and delivering results.
  • Collaboration and Team Building: Fostering a culture of teamwork and inclusivity to achieve shared goals.
  • Visionary Leadership: Inspiring and motivating employees by setting a clear direction and vision for the future.

In what ways does a lack of communication failure affect teams?

  • Misunderstandings: Poor communication confuses tasks, roles, or goals, resulting in errors and inefficiencies.
  • Unclear Expectations: Ambiguity in instructions or objectives causes team members to work at cross-purposes, reducing overall productivity.
  • Silos: Lack of open communication fosters isolated work environments where collaboration and knowledge-sharing are hindered.
  • Disengagement: Ineffective communication makes employees feel undervalued or excluded, decreasing motivation and engagement.
  • Inefficiency: Delayed or incomplete communication disrupts workflows and slows down project progress.
  • Distrust: Persistent communication gaps erode trust between team members and managers, undermining team cohesion.
  • Low Morale: A communication breakdown leaves employees feeling unsupported, resulting in diminished morale and satisfaction.
  • Conflict: Poor communication escalates misunderstandings into conflicts, negatively impacting team relationships and performance.

Do Mentoring and Coaching Strategies Differ in Their Approach to Managing Teams?

Yes, mentoring and coaching strategies differ significantly in managing teams, each serving distinct purposes and contexts. Mentoring focuses on long-term development by fostering a relationship-based dynamic where an experienced mentor provides guidance, knowledge, and empowerment to help mentees grow in their careers and navigate challenges. In contrast, coaching is task-oriented and centers on performance improvement through short-term, goal-specific interventions aimed at skill-building and addressing immediate difficulties. While mentoring emphasizes broad guidance and relationship-building, coaching is more structured, with clear objectives and timelines. Leaders adopting mentoring take on a supportive and advisory role, helping individuals align personal growth with organizational goals. In contrast, coaching requires them to act as facilitators, actively guiding team members in achieving specific outcomes. These differences highlight how both strategies complement each other in managing teams effectively.

What Are the Challenges of Managing Diverse Teams, and How Can They Be Addressed?

Managing diverse teams presents challenges in people management, primarily rooted in cultural differences, communication barriers, and unconscious bias. When cultural differences lead to misunderstandings and differing expectations regarding work styles, communication preferences, and decision-making processes, managers must be prepared to address strategy development effectively. A manager must foster cultural awareness through regular training and encourage open dialogue, promoting respect for each team member’s background. Language barriers are another common obstacle in multicultural teams, leading to miscommunications or feelings of exclusion. In a diverse team, offering language support or encouraging clear, concise communication ensures everyone is on the same page.

Unconscious bias influences hiring, promotions, and day-to-day interactions. Addressing these challenges in people management requires careful attention to career growth and unbiased decision-making to ensure equity in all processes. Conflict resolution also becomes more complicated in diverse teams, where values clash. Encouraging empathy and adaptability in leadership and promoting collaborative problem-solving helps address conflicts constructively. Lastly, maintaining team cohesion is often difficult in diverse groups, as varying perspectives might initially create divides. By actively promoting inclusivity, team-building activities, and fostering collaboration, managers can turn challenges into opportunities for growth and innovation.

What is the Best Way to Handle Employee Underperformance in a Team?

The best way to handle employee underperformance in a team is through a structured performance management approach that combines clear communication, support, and development. Performance management emphasizes the importance of identifying the root causes of underperformance—whether they stem from unclear expectations, personal issues, or lack of skills—and addressing them through open, non-judgmental feedback.

A well-implemented performance management system allows managers to collaboratively create a development plan with achievable goals, regular check-ins, and tailored training or resources. Continuous Performance Management techniques, such as frequent coaching and mentorship, help employees stay aligned with their objectives while making necessary adjustments. Incorporating tools like 360-degree feedback evaluations or Behaviorally Anchored Rating Scales (BARS) ensures objective assessments that enhance the development process. Maintaining a focus on motivation and accountability enables performance management to foster an environment where employees feel supported and are held accountable for meeting expectations. Addressing underperformance through empathy and clarity, a manager not only guides individual employees toward improvement but also safeguards team morale and productivity.

Over time, this erodes job satisfaction and loyalty, prompting higher employee turnover and absenteeism.

This results in missed opportunities, reduced innovation, and lower overall performance. In the long run, poor management practices create a toxic work environment that harms the company’s reputation and culture, ultimately undermining its competitive advantage and bottom line. Thus, Ineffective people management directly affects employee well-being and has far-reaching consequences on company success, hindering growth and organizational health.
